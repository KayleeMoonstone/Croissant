A croissant is a buttery, flaky viennoiserie pastry shaped like an Austrian kipferl but made using yeast-leavened laminated dough. Croissants are named after their historical crescent shape; the dough is covered with butter, rolled and folded multiple times, then rolled into a thin sheet using a technique known as laminating. The finished product has a layered, flaky texture akin to puff pastry.

Crescent-shaped breads and pastries have been manufactured since the Renaissance, and possibly since antiquity. The contemporary croissant was created in the early twentieth century when French bakers replaced the kipferl's brioche dough with a yeast-leavened laminated dough. The invention of factory-made, frozen, premade but unbaked dough in the late 1970s transformed them into a fast food that could be freshly baked by unskilled labor.

The croissant bakery, particularly the La Croissanterie brand, was a French answer to American-style fast food, and 30-40% of croissants served in French bakeries and patisseries in 2008 were prepared from frozen dough. Croissants are a popular feature of many European countries' continental breakfasts.

The History

The kipferl, the precursor to the croissant, dates back to at least the 13th century in Austria and appeared in a variety of shapes. Kipferl can be made simple or with nuts or other fillings (some people believe rugelach to be a type of kipferl). The croissant itself can be traced back to at least 1839 (some say 1838), when an Austrian artillery officer, August Zang, established a Viennese bakery ("Boulangerie Viennoise") at 92, rue de Richelieu in Paris.

This bakery, which sold Viennese specialties such as the kipferl and the Vienna loaf, quickly gained popularity and inspired French imitators (as well as the concept, if not the phrase, of viennoiserie, a 20th-century term for allegedly Vienna-style sweets). The crescent (croissant) shape of the French variant of the kipferl has become a globally recognisable shape around the world.
